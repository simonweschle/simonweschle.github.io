Competitor Lobbying and Regulatory Oversight.

with Benjamin Barber IV

A key problem for regulatory agencies is the information asymmetry between themselves and those they oversee. Previous literature has argued that corporations can exploit this and use lobbying to avoid scrutiny and fines for rule-breaking. In this paper, however, we argue that lobbying by industry competitors can be a countervailing force, as it allows an agency to better target its enforcement actions. We provide empirical support by analyzing fines assessed by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for 19,000 facilities over the years 2000-2013. Analyzing panel data using fixed-effects models, we show that competitor lobbying allows the EPA to target its enforcement actions. Facilities whose industry competitors lobby more are more likely to be fined by the EPA than facilities in industries that lobby less if their production involves many toxic chemicals, so violations pose a threat to human health. If they handle few toxic materials, and violations are therefore unlikely to result in serious harm, facilities in connected industries are less likely to be fined than those in non-connected industries. The companies' own lobbying has no independent effect. We also show that this effect only applies to facilities with competitors in close geographical proximity, indicating local industry knowledge as a mechanism.
