Competitor Lobbying and Regulatory Oversight.

with Benjamin Barber IV

How can a regulatory agency effectively check that laws are being followed? In particular, how can it overcome the information asymmetry between itself and those it oversees? In this article, we argue that lobbying by industry competitors provides information to the agency that it can use to target its enforcement actions more effectively. We provide empirical support by analyzing fines assessed by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) for 19,000 facilities over the years 2000-2013. Analyzing panel data using fixed-effects models, we show that facilities whose industry competitors lobby more are more likely to be fined by the EPA than facilities in industries that lobby less if their violations pose a threat to human health. If violations are unlikely to result in serious harm, facilities in connected industries are less likely to be fined than those in non-connected industries. This suggests that competitor lobbying allows the EPA to better target its enforcement actions within an industry. We also provide evidence indicating that local industry knowledge is the mechanism behind the findings.
